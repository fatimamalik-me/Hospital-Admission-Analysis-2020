# Hospital-Admission-Analysis-2020

Mental health disorders in urban populations are increasingly influenced by environmental stressors such as hous-
ing instability, chronic illness, and limited access to healthcare.
This study investigates the relationship between psychological
distress and real-world health outcomes in New York City using a
multi-dimensional data mining approach. We integrate three key
datasets: 

(1) a BRFSS-derived public health survey containing behavioral and demographic indicators

(2) New York State hospital discharge data filtered for mental health diagnoses

(3) ZIP-level income data from the U.S. Census.

Machine learning classification models, including Logistic
Regression, Random Forest, and XGBoost, were used to predict
mental health risk, with the Kessler Psychological Distress
Scale (K6) guiding the creation of the target variable. Logistic
Regression achieved the highest F1 score of 0.66, identifying
rent payment delays and unemployment as primary predictors of
psychological distress. Cross-dataset regression analysis revealed
a statistically significant correlation between regional income and
hospital admissions for mental illness, though income did not
predict the length of hospital stays.
These results highlight how behavioral and clinical indicators
can be integrated through data mining to support public health
decision-making. The findings may guide targeted intervention
strategies and improve mental health service delivery across New
York City communities.
